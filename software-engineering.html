Fantasy Sumo

For my final project of my Flatiron SE course, I built a Fantasy Sumo website!

I've been dealing with a troll on this website, and I'm pretty sure they found my public repo, so I had to make my repo private. If you're interested in checking out my code, send me an email (masonlynass@gmail.com) and I can send you a directory!

I've been meticulously compiling and arranging data about ozumo, professional sumo wrestling in Japan, since 2018.

Before I even considered making a website, I conceived of a set of rules and scoring system for my Fantasy Sumo game, and maintained multiple leagues at once in a Google Sheet.

With my newly acquired knowledge from Flatiron, I used React.js and Ruby on Rails to build a website for my game! Users can login or create an account, then draft a team of 7 wrestlers to root for in the upcoming tournament. User & team information is stored in PSQL database - in the future, users will be able to view their previous teams and compare how they've fared against other users.

My website has a comprehensive database of information, real world & game-specific statistics about all of the wrestlers - this database is the first I've found that allows users to sort by columns to compare wrestler stats.

I also made an account page to display scores for a logged-in user's team, and Rules & Scoring and Terminology pages to help newcomers draft promising teams and learn more about sumo!

In the near future, I'll switch to using an API my friend John has helped me build, so that I can retrieve wrestler and match information which automatically updates instead of updating scores and information manually.

Feel free to check out the Fantasy Sumo website - I hope you'll draft a team and follow the action!

Ice Honky

Ice Honky is an NHL Scoreboard website for goal horn lovers.

Whenever a team scores in real time, their goal horn sounds!

Ice Honky was developed with React.js, which easily handles constantly changing game data. The site requests data from an external NHL Scores API at a set interval whenever games are happening - we haven't found an API that allows WebSocket connections yet. Then we use the consistently refreshing data to display scores and game information.

If a team has scored since our last refresh, the site will display a modal that shows the most recent goal scored, and sound that team's goal horn. I like to use the site to keep track of game scores while working on other projects - I can just listen for a goal horn to know when my team has scored!

We've also set up a page with all of the goal horns, so users can hear any horn whenever they want, and try to find harmonious (or extremely dissonant) combinations of horns. We're using useSound, a custom React hook, to trigger audio events, whether they come from user input or a change in the data we receive from the API.

Ice Honky is a collaboration between myself and Alex Naughton, an excellent collaborator, full of fun ideas and ingenious solutions.

Better Bathroom Bureau

My buddy Alex and I worked together to build Better Bathroom Bureau - a website dedicated to helping people in Seattle access bathroom facilities around the city. We used React.js with some custom CSS to give it a "Craigslist-y" look on the frontend, and Rails to handle the backend and store all of our data.

Better Bathroom Bureau began as a Tumblr blog I started right after I moved to Seattle in 2015 - I reviewed bathrooms I visited out of 10, detailing highlights or lowlights in bathroom function, cleanliness, and style.

I had a great time working with Alex to create a full-fledged website version of BBB, and I'm excited to open up the bathroom discussion to other contributors and users around Seattle. This is the kind of resource that works best with a lot of user input!

Users can view bathrooms stored in the BBB database and leave reviews on bathrooms as a guest. Users can also login / create an account to contribute new bathrooms to the database, leave reviews tied to their account, and save favorite bathrooms for future reference.

Each bathroom submitted by users has its own page, with information about its description and accessibility, its location on a Google Map (thanks Google Maps API!), and reviews that site users have left about their experiences.

In the near future, we'll deploy the site and add a feature that shows "Bathrooms Near Me," cutting out some search time for users in a more urgent need of facilities. We hope that people around Seattle will contribute and the community will benefit from our hard work and creativity!

useSound

In March 2023, I gave a 10-minute talk about my favorite React hook: useSound, a custom hook for sound effects & web audio, developed by Josh Comeau.

In my React.js portfolio projects, I prefer to use useSound to play audio because of its simpler syntax and extremely accessible documentation. I like to use the hook to play audio inside event handlers, on form submissions, and wherever I think my site could use a little more character.

For this talk at the monthly SeattleJS meetup, I introduced the basic capabilities of useSound, then showed off some fun mini projects I made with it. I built a Random Note Generator to recreate the generative music I make in Ableton Live, as well as a keyboard-accessible drum machine and a very simple audio player.

Live Looping

I taught myself to live loop songs and different pieces of music,
using BinkBeats' binkLooper in Ableton Live.

    In order to identify, realize, and perform a complete piece of music without any pre-recorded MIDI pitch/rhythm data or pre-recorded audio stems, I developed a multi-step process:
  • I analyze the work to determine if the arrangement of musical phrases and song structure could reasonably be performed through looping. Musical phrases which repeat with some regularity, songs with similar chord structures throughout, and a lack of non-repeating elements occurring simultaneously are all good signs of a song you could loop.
  • Once I think a song could be fit to loop, I'll map out the song structure, orchestration in each section, and specific rhythms and pitches in each musical element. It's just another method of transcription!
  • Some detailed planning is required to turn a transcription into a programmable structure in Ableton Live. I need to decide when (in musical time) I'll record each element, making sure that I stick to my own physical limitations, and make sure to leave space for non-repeating elements or other things I need to perform in real-time.
  • Now that I have my performance structure in mind, I program dummy clips and automation in the binkLooper and other devices in Ableton Live. I can prescribe when instruments are recorded, when they begin looping, when they start and stop again, and how effects process the recorded MIDI data, and assign all of these functions to a specific, predictable timing.
  • With a little troubleshooting and practice, I can perform actions on MIDI devices or with physical instruments, which are recorded and played back in my prescribed framework, in order to perform complex arrangements of many musical elements independently (with help from my computer, of course)!

Check out this tutorial video I made about binkLooper to learn more,
or head over to the Audio Engineering page to watch videos of my live looping performances.

Unreal Engine / Metasounds

This was one of my first experiments in Unreal Engine's Metasounds;
I don't know much about Metasounds yet, but it seems like an exciting and inspiring tool for creative audio projects.

I was thinking of Brian Eno's Discreet Music and other slow ambient music, and I made a sort of primitive piece of music in which four wave generators produce random pitches that swell and fade in volume at their own pace.

I had already started to make procedural generative music in Ableton Live,and it was a puzzle to switch to a different user interface and reconfigure the rules in a new environment.
